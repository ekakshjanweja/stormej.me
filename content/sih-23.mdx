## Introduction

In the sea of hackathons, one of the most well-known in India is the [Smart India Hackathon (SIH)](https://www.sih.gov.in/sih2023PS?technology_bucket=QWxs&category=U29mdHdhcmU=&organization=QWxs&organization_type=QWxs).

I and my team **DelTechNeek** took part in our college's (Delhi Technological University) internal hackathon. There were 180+ teams and 1200+ participants in the internal hackathon itself. We worked on a problem statement for the Ministry of Urban Affairs (SIH1516):

> Suggest an AI-based solution to enable ease of grievance lodging and tracking for citizens across multiple departments.

<Callout type="note" title="TL;DR">
We didn't make it to the shortlisted teams — but we learned a ton and ended up solving a real-world problem.
</Callout>

## The Vision Behind GovBuzz

Introducing **GovBuzz**, an AI-powered chatbot for lodging grievances. It offers a simple voice command feature for language-inclusive access. GovBuzz processes complaints, filters spam, and directs them to the correct department. You get real-time updates and can participate in a user forum for transparent issue resolution.

## Tech Stack

### Mobile App

I was responsible for developing the mobile app, a critical component for user interaction. Our app required multilingual support and cross-platform functionality, leading me to choose [Flutter](https://flutter.dev/) for its versatility. For the database I opted for [Firebase](https://firebase.google.com/), driven by the need for speed during the hackathon.

Other technologies used in the app:

### Django Backend

Aryan took up the task of making the backend to connect the ML and the Flutter app via APIs. [Django](https://www.djangoproject.com/) was our first choice because the ML models were written in Python — integration became very easy. We also tried deploying the backend to [AWS](https://aws.amazon.com/), but couldn't achieve this in such a short duration.

### Deep Learning Models

Ayan and Aakhyat were responsible for creating the ML models. They collected the data from Google Forms and social media platforms such as Twitter and Facebook.

This data was preprocessed and then two models were created from it:

<Cards>

These models used [Tensorflow](https://www.tensorflow.org/), [NLTK](https://www.nltk.org/), and NLP techniques to process the user input.

### Admin Dashboard

Abhinav created a dummy website with a frontend. He used [React](https://react.dev/) and [Tailwind CSS](https://tailwindcss.com/) to make the admin dashboard fetch the grievances from the backend and route them to the concerned official/authority.

### Business Model

The team imagined a subscription-based model where each ministry or department could subscribe to our service. GovBuzz would then tailor AI models to their specific grievance data, facilitating quicker problem resolution.

## Why didn't we succeed?

Despite creating a full-fledged prototype and a complete problem statement analysis, these could have been some reasons we didn't make it through:

<Accordions>
  <Accordion title="Bad idea approach">
    While GovBuzz was a visionary solution, the approach might not have aligned perfectly with the specific requirements of the hackathon challenge. In hackathons, fine-tuning the idea and focusing on the core challenge statement can be critical.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Poor presentation skills">
    Effective communication is vital in hackathons. A less-than-optimal presentation could have impacted how the judges perceived the project's value and feasibility.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Less information about future prospects">
    Hackathon judges often look for projects with clear potential for future development and impact. Providing a robust vision for the post-hackathon future of GovBuzz may have been a missed opportunity.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Bad luck, maybe">
    Sometimes the cards just don't fall your way.
  </Accordion>
</Accordions>
